Matiure Rahman

The day was March 7, 1971

My brothers; Today I have appeared before you with a sad heart. You all know and understand. We have tried with our lives. But sadly, today the highways in Dhaka, Chittagong, Khulna, Rajshahi, Rangpur are stained with my brother's blood. Today the people of Bengal want liberation, the people of Bengal want to live, the people of Bengal want their rights. What I did wrong, in the election, the people of Bangladesh completely voted for me Awami League. Our National Assembly will sit, we will make the constitution there and we will build this country, the people of this country will be liberated economically, politically and culturally. But sadly, sad to say today, the tragic history of twenty-three years is the history of the oppression of Bengal, the history of the blood of the people of Bengal. Twenty-three years of history is the history of the cries of dying men and women; The history of Bengal is the history of painting the highways with the blood of the people of this country. I gave blood in 1952. Despite winning the election in 1954, we could not sit on the mat. In 1956, Ayub Khan issued martial law and kept us as slaves for ten years. My sons were shot dead on June 6, 1966 during the Six-Point Movement. After the fall of Ayub Khan in the 1969 movement, when Mr. Yahya Khan took over the government, he said, give constitution to the country, give democracy. We agreed. Then there was a lot of history, elections. I met with President Yahya Khan. I, as the leader of the Majority Party of Pakistan, not only of Bengal, requested him to convene the National Assembly on 15th February. He didn't talk about me, he talked about Mr. Bhutto. The first week will be in March, he said. We said, OK, we'll sit in the assembly. I said, I will discuss in the assembly; I have even said so far that if anyone speaks fairly, even if we are outnumbered, if there is one, we will accept his fair words. Mr. Bhutto came here, discussed. He said that the door of discussion will not be closed, there will be more discussions. Then I talked to other leaders, you sit down, we talk and make a constitution. He said if members from West Pakistan came here, the slaughterhouse would be the assembly. He said that whoever went would be killed. Shops from Peshawar to Karachi will be forcibly closed if anyone comes to the assembly. I said, the assembly will continue. Then suddenly the assembly was closed on the 1st. Mr. Yahya Khan called the assembly as president. I said I would go. Mr Bhutto said he would not go. 35 members came here from West Pakistan. Then it was abruptly stopped. The people of Bengal were blamed, I was blamed. People protested at gunpoint. I said, you observe the strike peacefully. I said, you shut down the factory. The people responded. People voluntarily took to the streets. They were determined to continue the struggle peacefully.

My golden Bengali, I love you,
forever your sky, your wind, my flute
